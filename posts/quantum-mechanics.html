Quantum Mechanics

Published on April 25, 2026 at 5:32 PM by isaiahnoelpulidosalazar

Quantum mechanics is the branch of physics that describes how nature behaves at very small scales—typically at the level of atoms, electrons, and other subatomic particles. Unlike classical physics, which governs everyday objects like cars or planets, quantum mechanics reveals a world that behaves in ways that often seem counterintuitive.

One of the central ideas in quantum mechanics is that energy, matter, and other physical quantities are often quantized, meaning they can only take on discrete values rather than a continuous range. This concept was first introduced by Max Planck in the early 20th century when he explained how objects emit radiation. Soon after, Albert Einstein applied this idea to light, proposing that light consists of particles called photons.

Another key concept is wave-particle duality, which states that particles like electrons can behave both as particles and as waves. This idea was famously demonstrated in experiments such as the double-slit experiment. When particles pass through two slits, they create an interference pattern typical of waves, even if they are sent one at a time. This suggests that each particle does not travel along a single, well-defined path but instead exists in a spread-out wave-like state until it is observed.

This leads to the principle of superposition, which states that a quantum system can exist in multiple states at once. For example, an electron can be in several possible positions simultaneously until a measurement is made. The act of measurement forces the system to “collapse” into one of the possible states. This idea is often illustrated by the thought experiment known as Schrödinger's cat, proposed by Erwin Schrödinger. In this scenario, a cat in a box is simultaneously alive and dead until someone opens the box and observes it.

Another fundamental principle is the uncertainty principle, formulated by Werner Heisenberg. It states that certain pairs of physical properties, such as position and momentum, cannot both be known precisely at the same time. The more accurately you measure one, the less accurately you can know the other. This is not due to limitations of measurement tools but is a fundamental property of nature.

Quantum mechanics also introduces the idea of probability rather than certainty. Instead of predicting exact outcomes, it provides probabilities of where a particle might be or how it might behave. These probabilities are described by a mathematical object called a wavefunction, which evolves over time according to the Schrödinger equation.

Another intriguing phenomenon is quantum entanglement, where two or more particles become linked such that the state of one instantly influences the state of another, regardless of the distance between them. Albert Einstein famously referred to this as “spooky action at a distance,” because it seems to defy the classical idea that nothing can travel faster than light. Entanglement has been experimentally confirmed and is now a key resource in emerging technologies like quantum computing and quantum cryptography.

Quantum mechanics is not just theoretical—it has practical applications that shape modern life. Technologies such as semiconductors, lasers, and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) all rely on quantum principles. More recently, scientists and engineers are developing quantum computers, which use quantum bits (qubits) that can exist in superpositions, potentially allowing them to solve certain problems much faster than classical computers.

Despite its success, quantum mechanics raises deep philosophical questions about the nature of reality, observation, and determinism. Different interpretations, such as the Copenhagen interpretation and the many-worlds interpretation, attempt to explain what the mathematics means about the real world, but no single interpretation has been universally accepted.

In summary, quantum mechanics provides a powerful framework for understanding the microscopic world. It challenges our intuitions, replaces certainty with probability, and reveals that reality at small scales is far stranger—and more fascinating—than everyday experience suggests.
